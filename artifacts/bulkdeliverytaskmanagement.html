Artifact Story

Situation

ScoopRoute administrators managed large volumes of delivery tasks, alerts, route exceptions, and status updates across connected applications. Because many workflows involved reports and bulk actions, they needed to quickly understand both what they were viewing and how a change could affect drivers, customers, or operations teams.

The existing copy in SR did not always make that clear. Some report titles were vague, and some action labels did not explain what would happen next. UX writing work was also spread across meetings and design reviews and handled by different teams, which led to similar content problems being handled differently across features.

Artifact Story

Task

I needed to make task and alert management clearer and easier to use. I reviewed tool titles, buttons, and supporting copy so admins could find the right information and understand the impact of an action before completing it.

I also needed a better way to record UX writing decisions so previous feedback could guide future designs.

Artifact Story

Action

Neptune, the product designer, regularly brought new features to me for copy review. I shared recommendations during design meetings, recorded final decisions in Jira, and maintained a Figma page that collected feedback across related workflows.

During each review, I checked whether report titles accurately described the content and whether action labels clearly explained the result. I added context when a change could affect a driver’s route, customer account, report, or another application. For higher impact actions, I made sure the consequences were visible before the administrator confirmed the change.

I used existing ScoopRoute documentation to keep terminology consistent and applied UX writing principles focused on clarity, context, and brevity.

Result

The work made the Delivery Tasks experience clearer and more consistent. Administrators received more descriptive report titles, clearer action labels, and better information about changes that could affect other parts of the platform.

The Figma page also became a reusable record of UX writing decisions. It helped me compare similar designs, identify recurring issues, and recommend more consistent patterns across future workflows.
