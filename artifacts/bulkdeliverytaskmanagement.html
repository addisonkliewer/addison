Artifact Story

Situation

ScoopRoute administrators and operations teams managed large volumes of delivery tasks, customer alerts, route exceptions, and status changes across several connected applications. The work often involved reports and bulk actions, so administrators needed to understand both the information on the screen and the wider impact of any change they made.

The placeholder copy did not always provide that clarity. Some report titles were too vague, and some action labels did not explain what would happen after selection. This became especially risky when a change made in one area could also affect a driver's mobile route, a customer account, or an operations dashboard. UX writing feedback was also scattered across meetings and design reviews, which made it easier for similar content problems to be solved differently from one feature to the next.

Artifact Story

Task

I needed to make bulk task and alert management faster, clearer, and easier to use. My immediate responsibility was to review report titles, action buttons, and supporting interface copy so administrators could find the right information quickly and understand the result of each action before they committed to it.

I also wanted to create a more consistent way to capture UX writing decisions. Rather than treating each review as a one time copy update, I needed to document recurring patterns and use previous feedback to guide future designs across related ScoopRoute workflows.

Artifact Story

Action

Neptune, the product designer, regularly walked me through new features and asked for copy feedback during design reviews. I often provided recommendations during the meeting, then added the final decision to the writer Jira tracker so the content could be followed through development. I also maintained a dedicated Figma page where I collected feedback from different features, compared similar screens, and reviewed earlier decisions before making new recommendations.

During each review, I checked whether report titles accurately described the information being shown and whether action labels clearly represented the operation that would follow. I avoided vague buttons when the action had a specific result, and I recommended additional context when a change could affect a driver's route, a customer account, a report, or another connected application. For higher impact actions, I focused on making the consequences visible before the administrator completed the change. I used existing ScoopRoute documentation as the primary source for terminology and applied principles from Microcopy: The Complete Guide and Strategic Writing for UX to balance clarity, context, and brevity.

Artifact Story

Result

The work improved the clarity and consistency of the Delivery Tasks experience. Administrators received more descriptive report titles, clearer action labels, and better information about changes that could affect drivers, customer accounts, and connected ScoopRoute applications. This made it easier for them to find the right information and understand what would happen before completing an action.

The Figma feedback page also became a reusable record of UX writing decisions. Instead of approaching each request as an isolated review, I could compare similar designs, identify repeated issues, and recommend more consistent solutions. The process showed how UX writing could support broader content strategy by connecting interface terminology with documentation, preserving decisions, and creating patterns that could scale across related workflows.
