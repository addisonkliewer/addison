Artifact Story

Situation

Documentation teams were beginning to use AI for research, content planning, release analysis, drafting, and knowledge base comparison.

The tools could help writers review more information and move through repetitive work faster, but they also introduced serious risks. Incomplete source material could produce confident but inaccurate output. Existing documentation could be contradicted without evidence. Product details could be invented, confidential information could be entered into unapproved tools, and generic drafts could appear complete before they had been properly reviewed.

The team needed a shared workflow that encouraged useful AI adoption without treating AI output as verified documentation.

Artifact Story

Task

I needed to define a practical AI assisted documentation process that explained what writers could delegate to AI, what information could be used, how sources should be evaluated, and where human review remained required.

The workflow also needed to support writers with different levels of product experience. New writers might need AI to begin with research summaries and outlines, while experienced writers might use it to produce a more complete initial draft from trusted sources.

In every case, the writer needed to remain accountable for accuracy, terminology, customer impact, and publication quality.

Artifact Story

Action

I organized the workflow around the complete documentation lifecycle, beginning with source collection and ending with publication records.

The research stage requires writers to gather trusted information, review existing documentation, evaluate source quality, and identify conflicts before asking AI to draft content. Existing published guides serve as the primary source of truth unless an SME, confirmed product test, or approved product change shows that they are outdated.

The AI research stage allows tools to summarize sources, compare release scope with the knowledge base, identify likely documentation gaps, propose questions for SMEs, and suggest affected articles. These outputs remain recommendations until the writer reviews and confirms them.

The drafting stage scales according to the writer's experience. Newer writers begin with source summaries, content plans, and outlines. More experienced writers may generate a complete initial draft when the product behavior and source material are well understood.

I added explicit guardrails against invented procedures, interface labels, permissions, test results, and specialized product information. Missing details must remain visible through questions, placeholders, or review flags rather than being filled with plausible language.

I also defined three human quality checkpoints. The assigned writer verifies facts and assumptions, a peer reviewer checks clarity and publication readiness, and the SME validates technical behavior, exceptions, and limitations.

The workflow includes confidentiality rules, approved tool requirements, source traceability, AI quality checks, and internal documentation of major decisions.

Artifact Story

Result

The workflow creates a repeatable way to use AI without weakening documentation standards.

Writers can move faster through source review, release analysis, content planning, and initial drafting while retaining control over the final result. Peer reviewers receive more complete and traceable drafts, and SMEs can focus their attention on unresolved technical questions rather than reviewing unsupported AI output.

The workflow also reduces the risk of hallucinated product details, conflicting terminology, generic content, confidential data exposure, and overreliance on AI confidence.

Most importantly, it makes responsibility clear. AI may help produce the content, but the writer and human review team remain accountable for what is published.
