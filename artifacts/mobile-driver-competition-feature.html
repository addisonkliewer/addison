Artifact Story

Situation

The ScoopRoute mobile team was developing a competition feature for delivery drivers.

The feature allowed drivers to join competitions, track delivery progress, review standings, and compare individual or team performance. It needed to encourage participation without confusing drivers with technical product language or unclear scoring information.

Drivers often used the app outdoors, inside delivery vehicles, and under significant time pressure. They needed to understand each screen quickly while moving between stops.

The feature also contained several possible states. A driver might have no active competitions, no recorded progress, no available results, limited access, or a temporary system error.

Placeholder copy did not always explain why the screen was empty, what the driver could do next, or how the current state differed from another competition state.

Artifact Story

Task

I needed to review and approve the mobile content before development began.

The content needed to remain concise enough for small screens while still explaining the driver's current state, available action, and expected result.

I also needed to ensure that screen titles, buttons, empty states, and error messages used consistent terminology across the feature.

The language needed to feel energetic and appropriate for a competition without becoming overly technical, confusing, or unnecessarily aggressive for delivery drivers.

Artifact Story

Action

Juventas, the product designer for the mobile experience, added placeholder copy to Jira stories and asked me to review each piece before development began.

I also participated in design and scoping meetings before the stories were finalized. My primary responsibility was copy review, but my documentation experience allowed me to provide broader feedback about the journey, terminology, and states that drivers might encounter.

During each review, I considered what the driver already knew, what action was available, what would happen next, and whether the terminology matched the rest of ScoopRoute.

I reviewed titles to ensure they clearly described the destination or task. I reviewed labels such as Join Competition, Try Again, View Standings, and Return to Route to ensure that each one accurately described the action that followed.

I designed empty states to do more than announce that information was missing. Each state needed to explain the current condition, set expectations, and guide the driver toward a useful next action.

I also reviewed error and access states. These messages needed to explain whether the problem was temporary, whether another path was available, and whether the driver should retry, return to the route, or wait for additional information.

For rankings and results, I focused on making points, completed stops, route progress, and competition status understandable without relying on internal scoring terminology.

I kept the language energetic enough to support the competitive experience, but I avoided technical descriptions that would slow down or confuse a driver who needed to return to a route quickly.

Juventas usually accepted my recommendations. When she selected a different approach, it was generally because she had additional design or product context as the subject matter expert.

Artifact Story

Result

The review created clearer and more consistent content across the mobile competition flow.

Drivers received more useful empty states, clearer action labels, and more understandable information about competitions, route progress, rankings, and results.

The development team also received approved content before implementation, reducing the likelihood that placeholder copy would remain in the released experience.

The project demonstrated how early UX writing involvement can improve more than individual sentences. Reviewing the complete set of product states helped identify missing guidance, inconsistent terminology, and unclear transitions before the feature was built.

It also showed how small mobile labels can carry significant product meaning. A short title or button must help a driver understand where they are, what they can do, and what will happen next within only a few words.
