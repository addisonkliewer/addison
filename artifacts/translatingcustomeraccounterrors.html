Translating Customer Account Errors into Plain Language

A fictional collection of account and billing messages showing how I translate complex system behavior into clear explanations, consequences, prevention guidance, and next steps for busy administrative users.

Artifact Brief

Key context for this sample

This artifact demonstrates my approach to error messaging, recovery guidance, and documentation support for complex SaaS account workflows.

The sample focuses on uncommon situations that could affect customer delivery services, subscription plans, account settings, or billing. These situations required more than a generic warning because users needed to understand what would change, how the change could affect the customer, and what action they should take next.

The examples are adapted from previous SaaS UX writing work and recreated using the fictional ScoopRoute product.

Artifact Story

Situation

ScoopRoute administrators managed customer accounts, delivery plans, billing, subscriptions, and service details. Some actions could create major changes, such as updating a customer's bill, changing an active plan, or affecting future deliveries.

Many of these issues were uncommon and difficult to explain. Placeholder messages often did not tell administrators what happened, what would change, or what they should do next.

Artifact Story

Task

I needed to turn complex system behavior into clear, concise messages. Each message had to explain the issue, the possible impact, and the next step without overwhelming the administrator.

I also needed to decide when the interface could explain the issue on its own and when a supporting help article was needed.

Artifact Story

Action

Juno, the product designer, contacted me directly or tagged me in Jira when a message needed review. I checked the placeholder copy, the workflow, and the expected system behavior. When needed, I worked with Product or Engineering to confirm how the action would affect billing, subscriptions, delivery plans, or customer reports.

I usually organized each message around a clear title, a simple explanation, the consequence, and a specific next step. I avoided technical language unless it helped the administrator solve the problem. I also avoided blaming the user and focused instead on explaining what prevented the action and how to fix it.

I documented my recommendations in before and after tables in Jira, then followed up with Juno in Slack. For more complex issues, I recommended an FAQ or help article that used the same terminology as the interface.

Artifact Story

Result

The revised messages gave administrators clearer recovery paths and helped them understand what had happened, what could change, and how to move forward safely.

The work also created stronger consistency between the ScoopRoute interface and its documentation. It showed that a good error message should do more than report a problem. It should help the user recover and avoid the same issue in the future.
