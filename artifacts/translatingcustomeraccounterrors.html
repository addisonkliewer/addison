Translating Customer Account Errors into Plain Language

A fictional collection of account and billing messages showing how I translate complex system behavior into clear explanations, consequences, prevention guidance, and next steps for busy administrative users.

Artifact Brief

Key context for this sample

This artifact demonstrates my approach to error messaging, recovery guidance, and documentation support for complex SaaS account workflows.

The sample focuses on uncommon situations that could affect customer delivery services, subscription plans, account settings, or billing. These situations required more than a generic warning because users needed to understand what would change, how the change could affect the customer, and what action they should take next.

The examples are adapted from previous SaaS UX writing work and recreated using the fictional ScoopRoute product.

Artifact Story

Situation

ScoopRoute administrators managed customer accounts, delivery plans, billing settings, subscriptions, and service information. Certain actions could create significant changes to a customer account. An administrator might update a setting that affected billing, changed an active delivery plan, altered a subscription, or produced an unexpected result in a customer report.

These situations were often uncommon edge cases. The underlying behavior could require deep product knowledge to understand, but the person encountering the message was usually a busy administrator who needed to make a safe decision quickly. Placeholder messages did not always explain what had happened, what would change, or how the administrator could avoid creating the issue again. A generic message could leave users unsure whether information had been saved, whether upcoming deliveries would be affected, or whether they should continue.

Artifact Story

Task

I needed to translate complex system behavior into concise interface content that helped administrators understand both the immediate issue and the larger consequence. Each message needed to explain what was happening, why it mattered, and what the administrator should do next without overwhelming them with technical details.

I also needed to determine when the interface could provide enough information on its own and when the issue required a supporting FAQ or help article using matching terminology.

Artifact Story

Action

Juno, the product designer for the account operations experience, contacted me directly and tagged me in Jira when an error or confirmation message needed review.

I reviewed the placeholder copy, the intended system behavior, and the point in the workflow where the administrator would encounter the message. When necessary, I worked with Product or Engineering to confirm what would happen to the customer account, subscription, delivery plan, or billing information.

I generally structured each message around five elements:

  1. A title that named the issue
  2. A plain language explanation
  3. The consequence of continuing
  4. Guidance for preventing the issue
  5. A specific action such as Continue, Save, or Review Changes

I replaced technical descriptions with language that reflected what the administrator could observe and control. I included the technical cause only when it helped the administrator resolve the issue or avoid repeating it.

I also avoided placing blame on the user. Instead of describing an action as invalid, I explained the condition that prevented completion and described how the administrator could correct it.

I documented recommendations in a before and after table within Jira so stakeholders could compare the original copy with the revised version and understand the reasoning behind the change. I then followed up with Juno in Slack to resolve questions or discuss alternatives.

When the issue required more context than the interface could reasonably contain, I recommended an expanded FAQ or support article. I used matching terminology so administrators could move from the message to documentation without having to reinterpret the problem.

Artifact Story

Result

My revised messages created clearer recovery paths for administrators managing sensitive customer account changes. Users received more useful explanations of what had happened, what information could change, and how to proceed safely. The messages also reduced ambiguity around billing, delivery subscriptions, customer services, and report behavior.

The collaboration created stronger consistency between the ScoopRoute interface and its supporting documentation. Designers gained a repeatable structure for future error messages, while Documentation gained clearer signals about which complex states required additional support content. The work also demonstrated how error messaging can support the complete customer journey. A strong message does not only report a problem. It helps the administrator understand the consequence, recover confidently, and avoid creating the same issue again.
