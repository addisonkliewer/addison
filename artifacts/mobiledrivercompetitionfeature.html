Shaping a Mobile Driver Competition Feature Before Development

A fictional mobile UX content sample showing how I reviewed titles, buttons, empty states, error states, rankings, and status messages for a competition feature used by ScoopRoute delivery drivers.

Artifact Story

Situation

The ScoopRoute mobile team was creating a competition feature that allowed delivery drivers to join challenges, track their progress, view standings, and compare individual or team performance. The experience needed to feel motivating without overwhelming drivers with internal scoring language or making the competition feel unnecessarily aggressive.

Because drivers often used the app outdoors, inside delivery vehicles, and between stops, they needed to understand each screen almost immediately. The feature also had several possible states, including no active competition, no recorded progress, unavailable results, limited access, and temporary errors. The original placeholder copy did not always explain why a screen was empty, how one state differed from another, or what the driver should do next.

Artifact Story

Task

I was responsible for reviewing and approving the mobile content before development began. The copy needed to fit comfortably on small screens while still helping drivers understand their current status, the action available to them, and what would happen after they selected it.

I also needed to make sure that titles, buttons, empty states, rankings, and error messages used consistent terminology throughout the experience. The language had to bring some energy to the competition while remaining clear, practical, and appropriate for drivers who needed to return to their routes quickly.

Artifact Story

Action

Juventas, the product designer for the mobile experience, added placeholder copy to the Jira stories and asked me to review it before the stories moved into development. I also participated in early design and scoping conversations, which gave me the context to look beyond individual sentences and consider the complete driver journey. During each review, I asked what the driver already knew, what they could do from the current screen, what would happen next, and whether the terminology matched the rest of ScoopRoute.

I revised titles and action labels so they clearly described the destination or result, including actions such as Join Competition, View Standings, Try Again, and Return to Route. I rewrote empty states to explain the current condition, set expectations, and point drivers toward a useful next step. For error and access states, I clarified whether the issue was temporary and whether the driver should retry, wait, or return to another part of the app. I also simplified how the feature described points, completed stops, route progress, rankings, and competition status so drivers did not need to interpret internal scoring terminology. Juventas accepted most of my recommendations, while occasionally choosing another direction when she had additional product or design context.

Artifact Story

Result

The review produced clearer and more consistent content across the mobile competition experience. Drivers received more useful empty states, more specific action labels, and easier to understand information about competitions, route progress, rankings, and results. The development team also received approved copy before implementation, reducing the risk that unclear placeholder content would make it into the released product.

The project reinforced the value of including UX writing before a feature is built. Reviewing the complete experience helped uncover missing guidance, inconsistent terminology, and unclear transitions that would have been difficult to solve one sentence at a time. It also demonstrated how much product meaning can be carried by a short mobile title or button. Within only a few words, the content needed to tell drivers where they were, what they could do, and what would happen next.
