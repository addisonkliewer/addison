ScoopRoute Tagging and Metadata System

A fictional taxonomy and metadata framework showing how I organize documentation by product area, audience, platform, skill level, language, and content type to improve filtering, findability, maintenance, and personalization.

Artifact Brief

Key context for this sample

This artifact demonstrates the information architecture and governance work behind a large SaaS knowledge base.

The system separates customer facing tags from internal content metadata. Customer facing tags help readers filter documentation according to their role, product, experience level, language, and area of interest. Internal metadata helps writers track ownership, review dates, lifecycle status, release impact, source changes, analytics priorities, and subject matter expert validation.

The model is adapted from taxonomy work originally created for FieldRoutes and recreated using the fictional ScoopRoute product.

Artifact Story

Situation

As the FieldRoutes knowledge base grew, customers needed a faster way to narrow hundreds of articles to the content most relevant to their role, product, industry, language, experience level, and current area of work.

The existing content organization had developed gradually across several years. Categories helped group articles, but inconsistent tagging could make filtering less useful. Freeform search keywords existed separately, and the system did not fully connect reader groups with search behavior or personalized content recommendations.

The documentation team also needed internal information for content maintenance. Writers needed to know which articles were current, who owned them, when they should be reviewed, why they had changed, and whether they had received technical validation.

Artifact Story

Task

I needed to create a controlled tagging model that supported customer navigation without turning every internal content attribute into a visible filter.

The system needed to distinguish broad categories from individual tags, separate search keywords from controlled vocabulary, and define how writers should classify content that crossed roles, products, workflows, or interface areas.

I also needed to create governance rules so writers could propose new tags without creating duplicate, overly broad, or short lived terminology.

Artifact Story

Action

I combined research developed with the Learning Experience team with the product and content categories I had built through several years of documentation work.

I organized customer facing tags into several major categories: Area, Industry, Language, Product Type, Role, Skill Level, and Content Type. These categories allowed customers to narrow content according to where they were working, which product they used, what role they performed, and how much product knowledge the content required.

I maintained the controlled vocabulary in Google Sheets and later transferred the approved values into Document360 so writers could apply them to published articles.

I kept freeform search keywords separate from the controlled taxonomy. Keywords supported alternate terminology and search behavior, while tags supported consistent article filtering.

I also added internal metadata for content ownership, lifecycle status, review dates, source of change, release impact, subject matter expert review, analytics priority, accessibility, localization readiness, and related articles.

The governance rules required writers to use approved values, apply the most specific accurate tags, avoid internal team names, review classifications during release updates, and propose new tags through Documentation.

A new tag could be approved only when it improved filtering, search, reporting, or maintenance, applied to several articles, represented a distinct concept, and did not duplicate an existing value.

Artifact Story

Result

The system created a more consistent way to organize and retrieve documentation across a large knowledge base.

Customers could filter content according to their product, role, experience level, language, and area of interest. Writers gained clearer rules for classifying articles and fewer opportunities to create duplicate or conflicting tags.

The internal metadata also created a stronger foundation for content audits, release planning, review scheduling, localization, analytics, and ownership.

The model supported future personalization by making persona and product information more structured. It also improved AI readiness by giving search and retrieval systems clearer signals about audience, subject, platform, and user intent.

Most importantly, the system treated findability as a content design problem rather than only a search problem. Better classification helped customers reach more relevant documentation before they had to refine a search manually.
