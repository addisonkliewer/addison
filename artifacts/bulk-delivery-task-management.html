Artifact Story

Situation

ScoopRoute administrators and operations teams needed to manage large numbers of delivery tasks, customer alerts, and route exceptions.

The system included reports, bulk actions, task statuses, and changes that could affect information across several connected applications.

Placeholder titles and action labels did not always make it clear what information a report contained or what would happen after an administrator selected an action.

This created a particular challenge for actions with consequences outside the immediate screen. An administrator might change a delivery task or alert in one area without realizing that the update would also affect a driver's mobile route, a customer account, or an operations dashboard.

UX writing feedback was also spread across individual conversations and design reviews. Without a shared record, similar terminology and action problems could be solved differently each time.

Artifact Story

Task

I needed to help make bulk task and alert management easier and faster.

My immediate responsibility was to review report titles, action buttons, and related interface copy. The content needed to help administrators identify the correct information quickly and predict the result of each action.

I also needed to capture recurring UX writing decisions so that previous feedback could support future designs.

The goal was not only to improve individual labels. It was to create greater consistency across the delivery task management experience and establish patterns that could be reused across related ScoopRoute applications.

Artifact Story

Action

Neptune, the product designer, regularly walked through new features with me and asked for copy feedback during the review.

I often provided recommendations immediately as we discussed the workflow. After the meeting, I added the decision to the writer Jira tracker so the work could be followed through development.

I also maintained a dedicated Figma page where I collected design feedback from different features. This allowed me to review earlier recommendations, compare similar screens, and identify patterns in the content problems appearing across ScoopRoute.

I reviewed whether report titles accurately described the information displayed and whether action buttons clearly represented the operation that would follow.

I avoided vague labels when the action had a specific result. I also recommended additional explanation when an action could affect a driver's route, a customer account, an operations report, or another connected application.

When consequences were significant, I focused on making those implications visible before the administrator completed the action. The goal was to help administrators make decisions confidently rather than discover the result afterward.

I used existing ScoopRoute documentation terminology as the primary source for product language so the interface and support content would remain aligned.

I also applied principles from Microcopy: The Complete Guide by Kinneret Yifrah and Strategic Writing for UX by Torrey Podmajersky. These references supported decisions about action labels, user expectations, context, and the balance between brevity and clarity.

As the Figma page grew, I began using it as more than an archive. It became a way to identify recurring patterns, recognize inconsistent product terminology, and apply previous content decisions to new designs.

Artifact Story

Result

The work improved the clarity and consistency of the Delivery Tasks experience.

Administrators received more descriptive report titles, clearer action labels, and better information about changes that could affect drivers, customer accounts, and connected ScoopRoute applications.

The Figma feedback page also created a reusable record of UX writing decisions. Instead of treating each request as an isolated copy review, I could compare similar designs, identify repeated issues, and recommend more consistent solutions.

This process demonstrated content strategy beyond copyediting. It connected interface terminology with documentation, captured decisions for future use, and created patterns that could scale across related workflows.

Most importantly, it supported faster and more confident task management. Clearer titles helped administrators find the right information, while clearer actions helped them understand what would happen before they committed to a change.
